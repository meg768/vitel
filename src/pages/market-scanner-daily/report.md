# Market Scanner Daily

Date: March 31, 2026

Edition status: First edition for March 31, 2026. Created on March 31, 2026.

## Front Page

- March 31, 2026 was a relief-rally session inside a still-stressed macro regime: equities bounced on signs Washington may accept a less-than-clean de-escalation with Iran, but the real driver remained the Middle East energy shock.
- This did not look like a clean risk-on day. U.S. and European stocks rebounded, while Asia still closed weak, the dollar stayed firm, and bond markets kept a stagflation message alive.
- What changed versus March 30, 2026: the market stopped pricing only immediate escalation and began pricing a possible pause, even though tanker attacks and Strait of Hormuz disruption were still active.
- Oil remains the key tell. Even after intraday pullbacks, crude is ending March with record-style monthly gains, which keeps inflation repricing and recession risk front and center.

## Cross-Asset Dashboard

- Global equities: U.S. stocks rebounded sharply by the close, with the S&P 500 up 2.3%, the Nasdaq up 3.2%, and the Dow up 1.9% on de-escalation hopes. Europe also stabilized, with the STOXX 600 and FTSE 100 each up about 1% intraday, but both regions are still ending a difficult month.
- Asia: the relief did not fully carry into Asia. Reuters reported MSCI Asia-Pacific was down about 0.5% on March 31, 2026 and Japan's Nikkei fell about 0.9%, capping the region's steepest monthly decline since 2022.
- Rates: the day's bounce in equities did not erase the month's bond damage. Reuters reported the U.S. 2-year Treasury yield was headed for roughly a 45 basis point monthly rise, its largest jump since October 2024, while German 10-year Bund yields were near 3%, their biggest monthly rise since late 2022.
- FX: the dollar remained the cleanest cross-asset stress signal. Reuters reported the dollar index touched 100.61 on March 30 and was still up 2.9% for March, its biggest monthly gain since July, while USD/JPY hovered near 160 and kept intervention risk in play.
- Commodities: crude stayed dominant. Reuters reported early March 31 pricing around $115 for Brent and about $106 for WTI, with Brent up roughly 59% in March and WTI up about 58%. Gold bounced to roughly $4,560-$4,580 per ounce, but Reuters still described March as its worst month in more than 17 years.
- Crypto: Bitcoin held near $67,500 on March 31, 2026 despite the geopolitical shock. That resilience is notable, but crypto still is not leading global macro price discovery the way oil, FX, and rates are.

## Abnormal Moves

- Oil: this remains the standout abnormal move. Reuters described Brent and WTI as heading for their strongest monthly gains on record or since 2020, depending on contract comparison. That is a genuine regime-shift move, not routine volatility.
- Asia versus U.S.: U.S. equities managed a sharp relief rebound, but Asian equities still ended the session weak. That divergence matters because Asia is more exposed to imported energy stress and is likely giving a cleaner read on second-order damage.
- Rates shock: global government bonds are still ending March with their steepest losses in years. That tells you the market has shifted from "war headline volatility" to "war-driven inflation and policy problem."
- Marvell Technology: shares jumped roughly 11% to 13% on March 31, 2026 after Nvidia invested $2 billion and expanded its partnership. That is an outsized single-name move with broader implications for AI infrastructure suppliers.
- Centessa Pharmaceuticals: shares surged after Eli Lilly agreed to acquire the company, another reminder that strategic biotech M&A is still active even in a hostile macro tape.

## Special Situations

- Unilever / McCormick: Unilever agreed to separate its food business and merge it with McCormick in a cash-and-stock deal valuing the unit at about $44.8 billion. Why it matters: this is one of the biggest consumer staples restructurings in years and sharpens Unilever's move away from slower-growth food assets. What to watch next: labor pushback, synergy delivery, and whether investors reward simplification over scale.
- Eli Lilly / Centessa: Lilly agreed to buy Centessa to deepen its sleep-wake and neuroscience pipeline. Why it matters: it shows strategic acquirers are still willing to pay for differentiated biotech assets even while macro risk is elevated. What to watch next: whether this reopens interest in adjacent CNS and sleep-disorder names.
- CoreWeave financing: CoreWeave secured an $8.5 billion delayed-draw term loan to expand AI infrastructure, bringing the last 12 months' financing commitments to about $28 billion. Why it matters: capital is still available for scale AI build-outs despite tighter macro conditions. What to watch next: financing spreads, supplier read-through, and whether private AI capex stays insulated from the broader risk-off tape.
- Nvidia / Marvell: Nvidia's $2 billion investment in Marvell and the deeper ecosystem tie-up is a reminder that AI infrastructure leadership is still being consolidated. Why it matters: in a shaky market, capital is clustering around a smaller set of strategic winners. What to watch next: whether this revives broader semiconductor risk appetite or stays concentrated in a few ecosystem names.

## Catalyst Calendar

- Wednesday, April 1, 2026: ISM Manufacturing PMI for March at 10:00 a.m. ET. This is the first major U.S. business survey after the March energy shock and should matter especially for prices-paid and new-orders detail.
- Friday, April 3, 2026: U.S. Employment Situation for March at 8:30 a.m. ET. The key question is whether labor data still look resilient enough for the Fed to stay patient even as energy pushes inflation risk back up.
- Monday, April 6, 2026: ISM Services PMI for March at 10:00 a.m. ET. Services inflation and demand resilience matter more if markets conclude the oil shock will be prolonged.
- Thursday, April 16, 2026: Euro area March HICP full release at 12:00 CET. Europe is the cleanest major-region test of imported energy inflation, so this will matter if today's flash rise proves sticky.
- Tuesday, April 28, 2026: Thomson Reuters shareholder meeting on its planned $605 million return of capital and share consolidation. This is a smaller macro event but a real capital-markets special situation to keep on the radar.

## Early-Info Signals

- The equity rebound is running ahead of confirmation from other asset classes. Oil is still historically elevated, the dollar still looks defensive, and bonds still reflect inflation stress.
- Gold bounced on March 31, 2026, but its worst month in more than 17 years suggests real rates and policy repricing have mattered more than safe-haven demand. That is not a comfortable macro backdrop.
- Europe looks more vulnerable than the U.S. if the energy shock persists. Euro area inflation re-accelerated in March while European growth was already soft.
- Asia's weaker equity performance versus the U.S. rebound is an early sign that energy-import exposure may be the more important lens than headline global risk sentiment.
- Bitcoin holding near $67,500 while small caps, Asia, and bonds still look fragile suggests liquidity is selective rather than broad. That is not yet a true all-clear.

## What Matters Most

- Highest-conviction change: the market narrative has shifted back to energy and war risk, displacing the cleaner soft-landing and rate-cut story that dominated earlier in 2026.
- Biggest unresolved risk: if Strait of Hormuz disruption lasts longer than markets now hope, March's oil shock turns into a more durable global stagflation problem, especially for Europe and Asia.
- Watch tomorrow: whether the April 1 ISM report confirms inflation pressure is spilling into the real economy.
- Watch next: oil first, then USD/JPY near 160, then front-end yields. Those remain the clearest stress gauges.
